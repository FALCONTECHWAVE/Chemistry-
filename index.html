Chemical Bonding

Definition: Chemical bonding involves the forces that hold atoms together in molecules.

Types of Bonds:

  • Covalent Bond: Sharing of electrons between atoms.
  • Ionic Bond: Transfer of electrons between atoms, resulting in the formation of ions.
  • Hydrogen Bond: A special type of dipole-dipole interaction involving a hydrogen atom bonded to a highly electronegative atom.

Key Concepts:

  • Hybridization: The mixing of atomic orbitals to form hybrid orbitals with different shapes and energies, which are more suitable for bonding.
  • Molecular Geometry: The three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a molecule, determined by the repulsion between electron pairs.

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) Theory: A model used to predict the geometry of individual molecules based on the number of electron pairs surrounding their central atoms.

Isomerism

Definition: Isomers are molecules that have the same molecular formula but different structural or spatial arrangements of atoms.

Types of Isomerism:

  • Structural Isomerism: Compounds with the same molecular formula but different connectivity of atoms.
    • Chain Isomerism: Variation in the carbon chain structure (e.g., butane and isobutane).
    • Position Isomerism: Variation in the position of a functional group or substituent on a carbon chain (e.g., 1-propanol and 2-propanol).
    • Functional Isomerism: Compounds with the same molecular formula but different functional groups (e.g., ethanol and dimethyl ether).
  • Stereoisomerism: Compounds with the same molecular formula and the same connectivity of atoms but different spatial arrangements.
    • Geometric Isomerism (Cis-Trans Isomerism): Occurs in compounds with restricted rotation around a double bond or ring (e.g., cis-2-butene and trans-2-butene).
    • Enantiomers: Non-superimposable mirror images of chiral molecules.
    • Diastereomers: Stereoisomers that are not enantiomers.

Coordination Compounds

Definition: Compounds formed from the coordination of metal ions with ligands (molecules or ions that donate electron pairs).

Key Concepts:

  • Ligands: Molecules or ions that can donate one or more pairs of electrons to a metal ion.
  • Coordination Number: The number of ligand donor atoms that are bonded to the central metal ion.
  • Chelation: The process by which a ligand forms multiple bonds to a single metal ion, creating a stable complex.

Types of Coordination Compounds:

  • Monodentate Ligands: Ligands that can attach to the metal at only one binding site (e.g., water, ammonia).
  • Bidentate Ligands: Ligands that can attach to the metal at two binding sites (e.g., ethylenediamine).
  • Polydentate Ligands: Ligands that can attach at multiple sites (e.g., EDTA).

Classification:

  • Primary Amines: One alkyl group attached to the nitrogen (e.g., methylamine).
  • Secondary Amines: Two alkyl groups attached to the nitrogen (e.g., dimethylamine).
  • Tertiary Amines: Three alkyl groups attached to the nitrogen (e.g., trimethylamine).

Properties:

  • Amines can act as bases due to the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom.
  • They can form hydrogen bonds, affecting their boiling points and solubility.

Hydrocarbons

Definition: Organic compounds consisting entirely of hydrogen and carbon.

Types:

  • Aliphatic Hydrocarbons: Straight or branched chain hydrocarbons (e.g., alkanes, alkenes, alkynes).
  • Aromatic Hydrocarbons: Compounds containing one or more aromatic rings (e.g., benzene).

Reactions:

  • Substitution Reactions: Replacement of a hydrogen atom in an alkane with another atom or group.
  • Addition Reactions: Atoms or groups are added to the carbon-carbon double or triple bonds.
  • Elimination Reactions: Removal of atoms or groups from a molecule, resulting in the formation of double or triple bonds.

Haloalkanes and Haloarenes

Haloalkanes:

Organic compounds containing one or more halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I) attached to an alkane.

Haloarenes:

Organic compounds containing one or more halogen atoms attached to an aromatic ring.

Reactions:

  • Nucleophilic Substitution: A nucleophile replaces a halogen atom in haloalkanes and haloarenes.
  • Elimination Reactions: Formation of alkenes from haloalkanes by the removal of a halogen and a hydrogen atom.

Biomolecules

Definition: Organic molecules that are essential for life, including carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.

Types:

  • Carbohydrates: Sugars and starches that provide energy and structural support (e.g., glucose, cellulose).
  • Proteins: Polymers of amino acids that perform a variety of functions (e.g., enzymes, antibodies).
  • Lipids: Hydrophobic molecules that store energy and form cell membranes (e.g., fats, oils, phospholipids).
  • Nucleic Acids: Polymers of nucleotides that store and transmit genetic information (e.g., DNA, RNA).
